A DRONE SURVEY: WHAT IS IT?

Let's define land surveying first in order to properly understand drone surveying. The science of identifying or re-establishing the borders, lines, corners, locations, distances, and monuments of real estate or land is known as land surveying. Using drones or unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) to collect data or information for use in land surveying is known as a drone survey. Drones equipped with LiDAR payloads, RGB cameras, and multispectral cameras may take several pictures of a survey location from various perspectives. Using photogrammetry software, the data can subsequently be used to produce 3D models or georeferenced orthomosaic maps of the project area. These maps can be used to obtain extremely precise volumetric measurements or distances by a field surveyor. Drones can produce high-resolution, extremely precise data in real-time at a lower cost than human aircraft or satellite imagery. Drones can also fly practically anywhere, allowing them to collect data in dangerous or inaccessible locations.

WORKINGS OF A DRONE SURVEY.

RGB and multispectral cameras on drones are used to take aerial photos of a project area from above the ground. LiDAR payloads are another element of drone systems that aid in data collection, particularly when determining an object's precise distance inside a specific area. During a land survey, a drone will fly over the project area and capture pictures of the land from various perspectives using its cameras. Every image that was taken has the precise coordinates of the location.

HOW ARE DATA COLLECTED BY DRONES?

Drones are mostly used to collect data from a Geographic Information System (GIS) computer system. They mapped out and visualized locations using this knowledge. Drones can, however, be outfitted with additional instruments for gathering data when doing field surveys. Drones used to be able to transfer data, but they could only record it. All of the data has to be retrieved by the operator. Drones may now send data in real-time and even convert unprocessed data into useful information thanks to GIS technology.

ARE DRONE SURVEYS ACCURATE?

The accuracy of drone surveys is a common concern for beginners in the field. However, it is important to note that drones are known for their high level of accuracy. In most cases, drone survey results can be expected to have an accuracy of approximately 2-3 centimeters horizontally and 5-6 centimeters vertically. By utilizing a top-of-the-line drone under optimal conditions, it is possible to achieve even greater accuracy, reaching levels as precise as 1 centimeter (0.4 inches) horizontally and 0.7 centimeters/pixels (0.3 inches/pixels) Ground Sampling Distance (GSD). It is worth mentioning that the accuracy of a drone survey is influenced by various factors, including the type of drone being used, the quality of the camera, the altitude at which the drone is flown, the nature of the ground cover, and the technology and methodology employed in geolocating the aerial images. Fortunately, obtaining highly accurate survey results is now easily achievable with the availability of high-quality commercial drones in the market.

WHY ARE DRONE SURVEYS BETTER COMPARED TO TRADITIONAL METHODS?

Drone surveys offer several advantages over traditional methods. Firstly, drones are capable of quickly capturing topographic data, making the process five times faster compared to land-based methods. This efficiency allows for significant time savings, as there is no need for additional manpower to achieve the desired survey outcomes. Additionally, the use of PPK geotagging eliminates the need for numerous ground control points, further streamlining the data collection process. Furthermore, drones have the ability to access and collect data from vantage points that are typically challenging or inaccessible to humans. Harsh terrains, steep slopes, and inaccessible areas no longer pose limitations when using drones for surveying purposes. This eliminates the need to close down streets or highways to perform surveys, as drones can capture data from above. In terms of data accuracy and comprehensiveness, drone surveying surpasses traditional methods. While traditional surveying methods, such as total stations, generate individual point measurements, drones produce a greater number of measurements that can be represented in various formats, including DTM, DSM, point cloud, contour lines, and orthomosaic. Additionally, each map generated by a drone contains 3D geo-data, providing a more detailed and comprehensive representation of the surveyed area. Despite these advantages, it is important to consider the disadvantages of using drones in surveying. One such drawback is the confusing and ever-changing drone regulations, particularly in cities or urban areas. Additionally, it is crucial to hire experienced and well-trained professionals to operate the drones, as inaccurate results may occur if operated by unskilled individuals. Moreover, drones may not be suitable for use in areas that frequently experience high winds, as this can affect their stability and data collection capabilities.

WHAT KINDS OF DRONES ARE USED FOR SURVEYING?

Various types of drones are utilized for surveying purposes. In the current market, there is a wide array of commercial drones available for purchase. When selecting a drone for surveying tasks, it is essential to opt for one that possesses the necessary features to effectively carry out the required job. While drones with multirotor configurations are common, those equipped with fixed-wing motors are more suitable for mapping surveys. Although operating a multirotor drone may be less complex, a fixed-wing drone is better suited for conducting surveys over extensive areas of land. Fixed-wing models have the advantage of prolonged flight durations compared to multirotor models, making them the preferred choice for large-scale surveys. Additionally, it is advisable to choose a drone with autonomous flight capabilities, as this allows for efficient and repetitive aerial mapping. In the absence of autonomous flight features, users can manually create a flight path using specialized software and program the drone for pre-determined flights. Given the necessity to cover vast expanses of land during aerial mapping, it is crucial to select a drone with sufficient battery capacity to sustain continuous flight operations. Opting for a drone capable of hovering for at least 30 minutes to an hour without interruption is recommended to avoid time wastage associated with frequent recharging or battery replacements. Furthermore, it is important to select a drone equipped with a high-quality camera capable of capturing 12 MP images and 4k videos for 3D mapping tasks. Such cameras are essential for photogrammetry applications, which rely on geotagged images to extract surface dimensions and generate detailed data sets.

WHAT KINDS OF DELIVERABLES CAN YOU ACHIEVE WITH DRONE SURVEYING?

Different types of deliverables can be achieved through the use of drone surveying, depending on the specific software, camera, and sensor being utilized. One such deliverable is a LiDAR point cloud, which allows for data collection in areas with dense vegetation or other obstacles. This point cloud provides a detailed and accurate model for measuring area, volume, and distance. Drones can also generate thermal maps, which can be used to identify areas with abnormal heat signatures. This is particularly useful for surveying cities, campuses, military bases, and other facilities. By conducting drone inspections, it is possible to monitor various infrastructure elements such as steam pipes, water mains, and condensation return lines. Another type of data that can be collected through drone surveying is a multispectral map. This technique involves capturing images within specific wavelength ranges on the electromagnetic spectrum. Multispectral maps are valuable for gaining insights into agriculture and crop management. Furthermore, drones can be used to produce 3D models of surveyed areas. By scanning the site and collecting relevant data sets, drones enable the creation of comprehensive 3D models using specialized software. This facilitates more in-depth site analysis, particularly for large objects like buildings and construction sites. Lastly, drones can generate 3D orthomosaic maps, which address issues with 2D projection. By capturing numerous images and compiling them into a 3D map, drones provide land surveyors with improved topographic data. These 3D orthomosaic maps can be integrated with Building Information Modeling (BIM) in construction surveys, allowing for the identification of discrepancies between the high-resolution 3D map and BIM objects at each construction stage.
